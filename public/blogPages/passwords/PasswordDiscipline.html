Password Discipline

Alternatively: Learn to understand why passwords are a difficult subject, and why you should start using better ones.

Image: (Haaland, 1999-2013)

We’ve seen it all before:

“Your password must contain at least 8 letters, one upper case, one number, and a plot with character growth and a twist ending.”

Why do site passwords need to be so complicated?  Couldn’t we live in a world where I just say “It’s me!” and I’m in?  Unfortunately, life isn’t so simple.  Websites need to know that it’s actually you when you try to log in, and they do that with passwords.

Why so complicated, though?

Hackers have been trying for a long time to fake their way past security measures so they can impersonate others.  Their reasons vary across the entire range of possibilities: from benign curiosity to malice, theft, and influence.  The primary tool at their disposal for getting into someone else’s account is guesswork.

What can I do about it?

Well, first things first: you need to learn about good passwords.  When hackers use guesswork to get into an account, the first thing they try is entering the most commonly used passwords.  Passwords like “aaaaaa” and “12345” are among the first ones they test.  30% of people have a password from the top 10,000 most common passwords (Atwood, 2017). Your first defence is to avoid that list.  

(Image: (Brooks, 1987))

Common password pitfalls:

These are some things you’ll want to keep in mind to avoid when making a new password:

  • Forgettable
  • Many users will switch in some capital letters, numbers, and symbols randomly into their passwords.  This can be sensible, but it leads to a rather dangerous problem: forgetting your password.  
  • Short
  • It can be a hassle to type out a long password, but a minor inconvenience to you can mean the difference between a secure account and a hacker seeing your banking information.
  • Reused
  • Many people like to use the same password (or extremely similar variants) to log in to most of their accounts.  Hackers try leaked passwords on other websites.  This is called Credential Stuffing.

The advice:

Thankfully, there are many steps you can take to protect yourself.  While ensuring complete security would have you taking steps that feel akin to living in a military bunker, here are some options with minimal hassle that can vastly improve your online defences.  

  • Start managing your passwords
  • There are many services designed for this exact purpose.  They can generate secure passwords instantly, and remember all of your passwords and where you use them.  
  • Enable two-factor authentication.
  • Not all services have this option, but it’s an added layer of protection. I recommend this video describing the benefits in greater detail.
  • Frequently change your passwords.
  • Changing your passwords every few months reduces the damage caused by unavoidable leaks.

In conclusion:

Passwords are a vital part of daily life on the internet and not enough people give them the attention they deserve.  Hopefully, this article has given you some tidbits of useful advice.  Protecting your account from unwanted intrusion isn’t a difficult task.
